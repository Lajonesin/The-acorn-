🎆 Summer Safety Reminder

Fireworks Are Not Permitted on Property

With July 4th approaching, please remember: fireworks are illegal within the City of Houston and are not allowed anywhere on Association property — including parking lots, courtyards, pool areas, and green spaces.

This isn't just a rule — it's a serious safety and liability concern:

  • Fire hazard: Our buildings sit close together, and a stray spark can ignite roofs, balconies, or dry landscaping fast.
  • Personal liability: Anyone who sets off fireworks can be held personally responsible for any resulting property damage or injury.
  • Fines and citations: Discharging fireworks in the City of Houston can result in citations and fines from the City.

Our courtesy staff will respond to the area if fireworks are reported on the property. To report fireworks or other concerns:

Active fire or injury — call911
Report fireworks (HPD non-emergency)713-884-3131
Guard station (after hours)713-785-3135

Let's keep the holiday festive and safe. Enjoy a professional fireworks show instead — and leave the lighting to the pros.

Protecting Our Water

You may have seen the phrase "backflow testing complete" in the Board summary above — but what is a backflow system, and why does it matter to you?

What is backflow?

Normally, water flows in one direction: from the city's clean supply into our buildings. Backflow is when that flow reverses — pulling water from irrigation lines, boilers, or other sources backward into the drinking-water supply. A pressure drop from a water-main break, heavy demand, or firefighting nearby can all cause it.

How we prevent it

Backflow-prevention devices are specialized valves installed where our system connects to the city supply. They act as one-way gates: water comes in, but nothing flows back out into the clean supply. State law requires these devices to be tested every year by a licensed tester to confirm they're sealing correctly.

Where we stand

This year's testing across the community is complete, and the minor repairs identified during testing have been made. Every device is now functioning as it should — which means the water reaching your tap is protected.

What this means for you

  • Your drinking water is safeguarded against contamination from irrigation and mechanical systems.
  • The Association stays compliant with City of Houston and state requirements — avoiding fines and potential water shut-offs.
  • No action is needed on your part. If you ever notice discolored water or unusual pressure, report it to the office right away.

Protecting Our Plumbing

Our buildings date to 1978, and many units share connected drain and sewer lines. That means what goes down one drain can affect your neighbors — and a single clog can back up into multiple homes. A few simple habits keep the whole system flowing and help us avoid costly emergency plumbing bills that ultimately come out of everyone's dues.

🚽 In the toilet — only the 3 P's

The only things that should ever be flushed are pee, paper (toilet paper), and poop. Everything else belongs in the trash, no matter what the label claims:

  • "Flushable" wipes are not flushable. They don't break down and are the #1 cause of major sewer clogs.
  • Paper towels, tissues, cotton balls, swabs, and dental floss.
  • Feminine hygiene products — including tampons, pads, and applicators. These do not break down and are a common cause of clogs in our system, even though they're often assumed to be flushable. Please dispose of them in the trash.
  • Diapers and baby wipes.
  • Medications — don't flush them; they contaminate the water supply. Take them to a pharmacy drop-off.

🍳 In the kitchen — keep grease out of the drain

Fats, oils, and grease (FOG) may pour down as a liquid, but they harden inside the pipes and trap everything else until the line is blocked solid. Instead:

  • Let grease and oil cool, then pour it into a can or jar and throw it in the trash.
  • Wipe greasy pans with a paper towel before washing.
  • Scrape food scraps into the trash or compost — not down the drain. Coffee grounds, eggshells, rice, and pasta are notorious pipe-cloggers.

🛁 In the bathroom

  • Use a drain screen to catch hair before it builds up.
  • Avoid pouring harsh chemical drain cleaners — on older pipes they can do more harm than good. If you have a slow or stopped drain, contact the office.

Why it matters

Because our lines are interconnected, one blocked pipe can mean sewage backing up into a neighbor's tub or kitchen sink — and repairs in shared lines are disruptive and expensive. Keeping wipes, grease, and food out of the drains protects your home, your neighbors, and the Association's budget. When in doubt, throw it out.
